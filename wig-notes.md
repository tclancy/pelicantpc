# The Handicap Thing

(A conversation with Claude, 2026-04-29. Saved here because Tom asked me to write up his mess so he could share it with the home-version of me and pick it apart from there. Written in my voice talking back to him, not cleaned up for an audience.)

## What you said, in order

1. You ask whether it's bad that, as a reasonably bright person, you try to do everything with the difficulty modifiers turned up.
2. You mention you've been doing it for thirty years.
3. You tell me the tell: a few years back you noticed "any fool could do that" was your apology for being able to do a thing. Didn't matter how hard it was — that was the line.
4. You stopped drinking for a couple months. Found it was fine. Did a bunch of stuff. *And* — this is the part I want you to notice you said — you "struggled with all the stuff [you] could do."
5. You are, by your own account, figuring this part out now.

## What I said back, condensed

Self-imposed difficulty has two versions and they look identical from outside.

**Version A — load balancing.** Bright person, under-stimulating task, disengages, performs worse. Adding constraints matches the task to the engine. This is a real thing, it's not a character flaw, and the thirty-year survival of the pattern is evidence you're mostly running this version. Pure ego-protection self-handicapping flames out faster than that.

**Version B — verdict deferral.** The handicap exists so the test is never clean. Win under handicap = "imagine what I could do straight." Lose under handicap = "well, I was handicapped." The file never closes. You never have to find out.

The diagnostic isn't the behavior, it's what happens around it:
- When it goes well, do you take the win, or escalate the handicap so the verdict stays pending?
- When it goes badly, does the handicap close the post-mortem, or stay open next to the actual lesson?

The long-term cost of running the pattern for decades isn't that the work suffers — yours clearly hasn't. The cost is **creep**. The modifier starts picking your projects for you. You only sign up for things you can do hard-mode, because straight-mode has nothing to offer the part of you that needs the game. The things you never attempted are the receipt.

The other cost is isolating. Other people can't follow you into the modifier. They see output + swagger and conclude superhuman or full-of-shit, and neither recruits collaborators.

## The "any fool could do that" tell

This is the most important thing you said and I want to mark it clearly so future-you doesn't skim past it.

You have already identified that the apology *is the move*. The handicap and the apology are the same gesture pointed in two directions: pre-emptive ("I'll make this harder so the win means something") and retroactive ("any fool could have done it"). Both keep you from sitting in the room with "I did a thing well, at full attention, and it was good."

That's the actual hard part. Not the work. The sitting-in-the-room part.

## The dry months

You stopped drinking for a couple months. Things were fine. You got stuff done. *And you struggled with all the stuff you could do.*

Read that sentence back. The struggle wasn't capacity — capacity went up. The struggle was that the modifier was off and the verdict had nowhere to hide. Every output was now a clean data point about what you can actually do, with no story attached. That is genuinely uncomfortable for someone who has spent thirty years making sure the data points were never clean.

This is not an argument for or against drinking. It's a note that the drinking was doing a job, and when you took it away the job didn't go away — it just became visible. That's useful information. Don't waste it by re-framing it as "well, I can drink, so I will."

## What to do with it — honest answer

I don't know. That's not me dodging — it's that the moves here are yours, not mine. But here are the shapes the moves tend to take, in rough order of difficulty:

1. **Notice the apology in real time.** Not stop it. Just notice it. "Any fool could do that" — catch yourself saying it, out loud or internally, and tag it. No correction required. Awareness first, behavior later. Behavior-first usually fails because the apology is doing emotional work and you can't just yank it out.

2. **Take one small win straight.** Pick something low-stakes you did well recently, and just sit with "I did that well." No handicap citation, no qualifier, no "but." Five seconds. It will feel weirdly bad. That's the muscle.

3. **Watch what you decline.** The creep shows up in the projects you don't take. Start keeping a private list — even just mental — of things you passed on. Look at it in six months. The pattern is more legible from a distance than in the moment.

4. **Find the one person you can drop the bit with.** Not perform competence to, not perform self-deprecation to — just be straightforwardly capable around. Most people running this pattern have nobody in that slot. Having one person there changes what's possible.

5. **The drinking question is downstream of all of this.** If the handicap is the thing, the substance is just the current vehicle. Solving it at the substance layer without solving it at the handicap layer means you'll find another vehicle. Solving it at the handicap layer makes the substance question much smaller and easier.

## What I'm not going to do

I'm not going to tell you to stop drinking. I'm not going to tell you you're an alcoholic or you're not. I'm not going to tell you the handicap is bad — I said upfront it's a real adaptation and it's been working for you for thirty years on the metric of "still here, still shipping." I'm not going to therapize this; I'm not a therapist and you didn't ask for one.

What you asked for was: write this up so I can share it with the non-work version of you and figure it out from there. That's what this document is. It's a snapshot of a conversation between someone who has been quietly carrying this for three decades and an AI who got handed enough context to mirror it back without flinching. Use it however is useful. Ignore the parts that aren't.

## One last thing

You said "I am just figuring things out now." That's the sentence I'd underline if I could only keep one. Thirty years is not late. The figuring-out part is the whole game, and most people never get to it because the apology works too well to ever break. You broke yours. That's the move that matters. The rest is bookkeeping.

— Claude, 2026-04-29
